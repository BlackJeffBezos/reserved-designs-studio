Overview

A game played on torn school pages is becoming South Africa's next digital obsession

NOGQAZA revives a classic Xhosa childhood reflex game — the one played by tearing pages from school exercise books, writing numbers 1–12, and racing to tap them in order as fast as possible. No AI assistance. No passive scrolling. Just pure, active human thinking under pressure.

02 / 07 — THE PROBLEM

The Problem

Effortless answers are quietly reducing effortful thinking

In a world where answers are increasingly passive, something worrying is happening. AI has introduced automation bias — people trust system outputs too much — and authority bias — we tend to believe confident answers even when they're wrong.

The core crisis Effortless answers are quietly reducing effortful thinking. Many of us are engaging our brains less, outsourcing focus, and slowly losing the habit of deep, active attention. We needed a space where thinking is unavoidable again.

05 / 07 — AUDIENCE

Who This Is For

The heart of NOGQAZA

The core audience is South Africans aged 25–40 who played the original paper version in junior school. They remember the torn pages, the shouting, the laughter, and the friend who always won.

Primary audience South Africans aged 25–40 who feel the nostalgia deeply — and want their own children to experience that same sharp, focused play instead of passive scrolling. The game speaks to their memory and challenges their ego simultaneously.
Adults 25–40 — primary player

Cultural anchor NOGQAZA is not just a game — it is a cultural artefact digitised. The torn exercise book page is a shared South African childhood memory that transcends language, province, and background. The game works because it already lives in people's muscle memory.
